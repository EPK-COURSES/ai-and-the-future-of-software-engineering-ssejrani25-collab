# Personal Reflection


---

I came into this research already feeling positive about AI in software engineering, and honestly, what I found confirmed that view rather than challenged it. But confirmation is not the same as having nothing new to think about. Some things still landed differently once I looked at them more carefully.

**What excited me going in** was the obvious part: AI tools are genuinely powerful. The idea that I can describe a function in plain language and get working code back in seconds, or that I can paste an error message and get a reasonable debugging suggestion instantly — that is not a threat to me, it is a tool. I have always thought of it that way, and the research backed that up. Productivity gains are real. The engineers using these tools well are getting more done, not getting replaced.

**What surprised me** was not a dramatic reversal of that view, but a sharper understanding of *why* the human side still matters. I expected the answer to be something like "AI cannot be creative" or "AI does not understand context yet." Those things are true, but what I found more compelling is this: AI cannot be responsible. It cannot be accountable. When software causes harm — a data breach, a biased decision, a system that fails vulnerable users — someone has to answer for it. That someone is always human. That point stayed with me more than I expected.

**My biggest concern** is still about jobs, and I want to be honest about that rather than pretend I only think about abstract ethics. The question of whether there will be enough positions for software engineers five or ten years from now is real, and I do not think anyone can answer it with certainty. The research I found does not make that fear disappear. What it does is reframe it slightly. The engineers most at risk are not engineers in general — they are engineers who do only the parts of the job that AI can replicate: writing boilerplate, translating simple requirements into code, performing repetitive debugging tasks. Engineers who develop the skills that sit around and above that — problem decomposition, system design, team leadership, ethical judgment — are harder to automate.

That is not a guarantee. But it is a direction.

**The skill I keep coming back to is critical thinking.** Not because it sounds impressive, but because it is the thing that actually makes everything else work. You cannot communicate well without thinking clearly about what you actually mean. You cannot design a good system without questioning your assumptions about what the system needs to do. You cannot use AI tools responsibly without being able to evaluate whether the output they give you is correct. Critical thinking is not one skill among many — it is the foundation everything else is built on.

**How will I personally adapt?** A few things feel concrete to me after this research. First, I want to make sure I always understand the code I ship, whether I wrote it or an AI did. The habit of reading, questioning, and testing output — not just trusting it — is something I want to build early. Second, I want to practice explaining my reasoning, not just producing results. Documentation, code reviews, technical conversations — these are places where the "why" matters as much as the "what," and that is a very human skill. Third, I want to stay genuinely curious about how systems work at a level deeper than what any single tool operates at.

**My opinion did not change dramatically** through this research, but it became more grounded. I went in thinking AI was an opportunity. I still think that. But now I understand more specifically what I need to develop to make sure it stays an opportunity for me personally, rather than a threat.

The engineers who will do well in this era are the ones who treat AI as a collaborator and themselves as the professional responsible for the outcome. That is the kind of engineer I am working toward being.
